DESIGN NATURE, FALL 2011



SUMMARY
Meet the cutest elephant to ever roll around on castor wheels. We designed and built a biologically-inspired toy for fourth graders that mimicked the curl of an elephant's trunk. Our design goal was to build a toy that aesthetically looked like an elephant and allowed the child to control the trunk's movements.

THE TEAM
Rachel Boy, Jessica Sutantio, Michael Warner

THE SKILLS
Design, Prototyping, Solidworks, Machine Shop, Interaction with Users
THE DETAILS

Our final elephant toy had three functions. (1) Children were able to ride on its back. (2) The trunk could be curled up and down. (3) The elephant could take in and store water and then shoot it back out.

We learned that children are drawn to toys they can ride on, so we decided to make our elephant large enough to carry a small child. The body of our elephant toy was built from wood, and we assembled the head frame and trunk using laser-cut delrin and flexible plastic. The trunk was threaded using fishing wire, which was then attached to a handle. The fishing line would curl as the handle was pulled, creating tension in the fishing line and bending the plastic to move the trunk.

Our toy ended up being a glorified water gun. We ran tubes through the trunk of the elephant and created a pump and tank system. The children would dip the trunk into a bucket of water and use one handle to pump water into the elephant, and then use another handle to shoot the water out.

In order to ensure the safety of the fourth graders riding our toy, we attached long beams to stabilize the elephant before adding castors. The children could then ride the elephant around without the danger of tipping over.

We also designed a game for the fourth graders, providing them with targets on a smaller model of an elephant made of foam. If they were able to accurately shoot water at the targets, they would gain points and succeed in giving the baby elephant a bath. Our elephant was a huge hit with the fourth graders on demo day.

SOFTWARE DESIGN, SPRING 2012


SUMMARY
We built a website called Sleepstalker that allows users to record the amount of sleep they get each night. Users can track, analyze and share their sleep patterns with their friends and create groups to view statistics.

THE TEAM
Ingrid H-K, Rachel Mathew, Alec Radford, Mary Ruthven

THE SKILLS
UX Design, HTML/CSS, Django
THE DETAILS

Our idea stemmed from a project started by our classmates who were using a Google Spreadsheet to log the number of hours of sleep they got each night. As data was collected, statistics were calculated and graphs were created. This inspired us to create a website that extends the idea of sharing sleep patterns.

Our main goal was to design a website that allowed for individual and group management of sleep pattern data in an aesthetically pleasing way. In Sleepstalker, each user has their own sleep diary where they record the number of hours of sleep they get each night. This data is used to display statistics pertaining to the users' sleeping patterns, both as individuals and in context of groups.

Below are two screenshots from our website. The one on the left shows the user profile page, where users can view their individual sleep trends. The one on the right shows how users can log the number of hours of sleep they get per night.

SOFTWARE SYSTEMS, FALL 2012

SUMMARY
Sandbox is an internal file system at Olin College that allows files to be stored by users for a maximum of thirty days. Our team characterized the workload on Sandbox, and used this characterization to determine a good policy for file deletion.

THE TEAM

(from left to right) Kevin O'Toole, Ariana Chae, Prof. Allen Downey, Rachel Boy, Kyle McConnaughay, Mary Ruthven

THE SKILLS
Python, MATLAB, Cache Algorithms and Replacement Policies
THE DETAILS

A history of file access was accumulated using file metadata on Sandbox, Olin's internal file system. We wrote a Python script to collect file metadata, taking measurements over the course of a few weeks.

We then compared four mathematical models of file access to determine the best fit for file access times and found that the probability of access decreases exponentially with time. Using this model of file accesses, we created a synthetic simulation of file use to analyze five file deletion policies: the current fixed-time deletion policy, three policies which use access history to better predict future access, and an omniscient policy which perfectly predicts future access.

We created a simulation with our model projected across a six-month period to determine which policy most accurately saves files when they are needed. Additionally, we evaluated these algorithms on thirty days of real access information of Sandbox files. In order to measure the performance of the various policies, we evaluated them based on maximum size used and number of failed accesses.

We found that our three new policies (least recently used, scored exponential decay and scored linear decay) each performed much better than the current policy. Of all three policies, scored linear decay performed the best overall, and also shows the most promise of guaranteeing file preservation for the user under certain conditions.

We submitted an abstract and a poster entitled, "Workload Characterization and Replacement Policies for a Shared Network Drive" to the 2013 Consortium for Computing Sciences in Colleges -- Northeastern Region and won first place in the Undergraduate Poster and Research Competition.

PRINCIPLES OF ENGINEERING, SPRING 2013



SUMMARY
We designed and built an art generator that allows users to use an Android application on their smartphone to draw a simple sketch, after which they can take a long exposure photograph of our moving LED display box to produce a light photograph of their sketch.

THE TEAM
Helen Lyons, Casey Monahan, Wonho Ryu, Jongchan Woo

THE SKILLS
Arduino with Bluetooth, Android SDK, Circuits
THE DETAILS

Our goal was to design and create an art generator that would integrate software, electrical and mechanical components.

On the software side, we built an Android application that took a sketch drawn by a user and sent it via Bluetooth to an Arduino. On the electrical side, we built a circuit that allowed the Arduino to control a bridge of LEDs and a DC motor. On the mechanical side, we devised a frame and gantry that incrementally moved the row of LEDs downwards. By lighting up certain LEDs based on the position of the row of LEDs, we were able to recreate the sketch one row at a time. Taking a long exposure photograph as the row of LEDs moved would combine all of these rows to reproduce the original sketch.

This video shows our system in action. We covered our circuit with cloth for our final demonstration for aesthetic reasons, but left it uncovered in the video to allow people to see how our system was put together.

More information can be found on our project website and our code can be found on Github.
